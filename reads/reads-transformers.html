Why Transformers Scale Better Than We Thought

The attention mechanism was supposed to be a bottleneck. Instead it became the foundation of everything. Here's the geometry that explains why — and what it means for what comes next.

By Least Squares

The Quadratic Problem Everyone Keeps Misquoting

Every few months, a new paper declares that attention is O(n²) and therefore doomed. The argument is clean: for a sequence of length n, every token must attend to every other token, producing an n×n matrix. Double the sequence length and you quadruple the compute. At 100k tokens that's 10 billion pairs per layer.

The problem is that this framing confuses worst-case compute with practical throughput. The attention matrix is dense in theory, but most of its entries are near-zero after softmax — they carry almost no signal. The real question was never whether attention is quadratic. It was whether the quadratic term is the bottleneck in practice.

Key insightThe attention bottleneck only bites at sequence lengths beyond ~32k tokens. For most tasks, the FFN layers are the actual compute bottleneck — not attention.

What Actually Governs Transformer Scaling

The scaling laws literature — from Kaplan et al. to the Chinchilla paper — consistently finds that model performance scales predictably with three variables: compute budget, number of parameters, and dataset size. Sequence length appears almost nowhere in these curves.

This is not an accident. The transformer's expressiveness comes primarily from depth and width, not context length. More layers give more composition; more heads give richer subspace decompositions. A 70B parameter model with 4k context beats a 7B model with 128k context on nearly every benchmark. The architecture scales with parameters, not sequence length.

The Residual Stream View Changes Everything

The cleanest mental model for why transformers scale is the residual stream interpretation. Every token is represented as a single vector that flows through the network. Each attention head and each FFN layer reads from that vector, computes a small update, and adds it back.

This means the model is not doing a single monolithic computation — it's running hundreds of small, composable operations in parallel across the depth of the network. Each head specialises. Some heads track syntactic structure. Some track coreference. Some track positional relationships. None of them need to solve the full problem — they each need to solve one small piece.

The transformer is not one model. It's several hundred small models that have learned to cooperate by writing to a shared scratchpad.

Why This Matters for Linear Attention

Models like Mamba and RWKV replace the attention matrix with a recurrent state that has O(n) complexity. They work surprisingly well — but they work by approximating the attention operation, not by replacing what attention does architecturally. The residual stream, the specialised heads, the composable updates: all of that is still present.

The scaling advantage of transformers isn't in the attention equation. It's in the residual structure — a design that lets the model throw more parameters at any part of the computation without disrupting everything else. That property survives the move to linear attention.

What Comes Next

The frontier is hybrid architectures — models that use full attention for short-range composition and linear recurrence for long-range memory. The intuition: most useful context is local, so most layers can be linear. A handful of full-attention layers at strategic depths preserve the long-range expressiveness that matters.

If that bet is right, the next generation of frontier models won't look like "transformers vs SSMs" — they'll look like carefully tuned mixtures of both, with the ratio optimised per layer and per task. The quadratic bottleneck will stop being a binary constraint and become a knob you tune.
