The Hidden Costs of RAG in Production

RAG demos are easy. Production RAG is a latency budget problem wrapped in an accuracy problem, wrapped in an infrastructure problem. Here's where the costs actually hide.

Every RAG prototype feels fast. You embed a few documents, throw them into a vector store, wire up a retrieval call, and the LLM produces a grounded answer in a second or two. Ship it.

Then you add real documents. Real users. Real query distributions. And suddenly the system that felt snappy in development has a p95 latency of 4.2 seconds, a retrieval precision that degrades on anything outside your eval set, and an infrastructure bill that scales super-linearly with usage.

Here's where the costs are hiding — and what to do about them.

The RAG Latency Stack

A naive RAG pipeline has four serial hops, each with its own latency floor:

  1. Query embedding — encode the user query into a vector (5–30ms)
  2. Vector retrieval — approximate nearest-neighbour search over your index (5–50ms)
  3. Context assembly — fetch, re-rank, and truncate the retrieved chunks (10–100ms)
  4. LLM generation — the model generates a response over the assembled context (500ms–5s)

Steps 1–3 are fast enough in isolation. The problem is they're serial, they add latency variance, and step 3 in particular is deceptively expensive at scale.

Re-ranking: The Hidden Bottleneck

In any serious deployment, you'll add a cross-encoder re-ranker between retrieval and generation. Bi-encoder retrieval (the embedding search) is fast but imprecise — it ranks by cosine similarity in a shared embedding space, which doesn't model query-document interaction directly.

A cross-encoder re-ranker takes each (query, document) pair and scores it jointly, which is dramatically more accurate. It's also dramatically more expensive: O(k) forward passes through a smaller model, where k is your number of retrieved candidates.

Rule of thumb: Retrieve top-50 with your bi-encoder, re-rank to top-5 with your cross-encoder. The cross-encoder adds 50–200ms at typical document lengths on a GPU, but precision gains often justify it — especially if the downstream LLM call is expensive.

Semantic Caching

The highest-leverage latency optimisation in most production RAG systems isn't faster retrieval — it's not doing the retrieval at all. Semantic caching stores (query_embedding → response) pairs and serves cached responses for semantically similar queries.

The implementation is straightforward: embed the incoming query, do a nearest-neighbour lookup in a cache index, and return the cached response if similarity exceeds a threshold (typically 0.92–0.97 cosine similarity). A cache hit costs ~10ms instead of ~2000ms.

Cache hit rates vary wildly by domain. Internal knowledge bases with repetitive query patterns (support bots, internal search) see 40–70% hit rates. Open-ended consumer products see 5–20%. The economics only work if your hit rate justifies the cache infrastructure cost.

The staleness problem

Semantic caches are hard to invalidate. When source documents update, you need to identify and expire every cached response that drew on those documents — which means tracking provenance at the chunk level. Most teams skip this and accept some staleness. That's fine until it isn't.

Long Context vs. RAG

A genuine architectural question has emerged: as context windows stretch to 128k–1M tokens, does RAG still make sense? Why not just stuff everything into the prompt?

The honest answer is: it depends on your corpus size and your latency requirements.

  • Corpus under ~500 pages? Long-context may be simpler and equally accurate. No retrieval infra to maintain, no chunking decisions to agonise over.
  • Corpus in the millions of documents? No context window is large enough. RAG is the only option.
  • Latency-sensitive applications? Long-context inference scales poorly with input length. A 100k-token prompt generates slowly and costs significantly more per call than a 2k-token RAG context.
Long context is a great escape hatch. It's not a replacement for a well-designed retrieval system at scale.

The Chunking Problem Nobody Talks About

Retrieval quality is dominated by one decision that most tutorials gloss over: how you chunk your documents. Fixed-size token windows are the default and they're usually wrong — they split sentences, discard context, and create chunks that are semantically incoherent.

Better strategies, roughly in order of effort and payoff:

  • Sentence-aware splitting — respect sentence boundaries, don't split mid-thought
  • Semantic chunking — embed sentences and split on embedding-space discontinuities
  • Document-structure aware — use headings, sections, and metadata as natural boundaries
  • Hierarchical chunking — small chunks for retrieval, large parent chunks fed to the LLM

Hierarchical chunking is particularly underused. Retrieve at fine granularity (sentence or short paragraph) for precision, but feed the parent section to the LLM for context. It combines the precision of small chunks with the coherence of large ones.

A Production Checklist

Before you launch a RAG system, make sure you have answers to:

  • What's your p95 latency budget, and which stage is your current bottleneck?
  • Do you have an offline eval set with ground-truth (query, relevant_chunks) pairs?
  • How do you handle document updates? What's your re-indexing strategy?
  • Is semantic caching worth it for your query distribution?
  • Have you measured retrieval precision at your chosen k? Not just end-to-end accuracy?

RAG is not a pattern you deploy once. It's an ongoing calibration between retrieval precision, latency, and infrastructure cost. The teams that treat it that way build systems that compound in quality. The ones that don't keep rewriting their chunking strategy every quarter.
