Why Transformers Scale Better Than We Thought

The quadratic attention bottleneck was supposed to be a dealbreaker at scale. It wasn't — and understanding why reveals something deep about how information flows through these models.

When the original "Attention Is All You Need" paper dropped in 2017, critics were quick to point out a fundamental flaw: self-attention is O(n²) in sequence length. Double the tokens, quadruple the compute. At the scales we're running today — hundred-thousand token context windows — that should have been a death sentence.

It wasn't. And unpacking why tells us a lot about what transformers are actually doing under the hood.

The Quadratic Problem, Precisely

In standard multi-head attention, every token attends to every other token. For a sequence of length n, that's n² attention scores computed per layer, per head. Memory scales the same way — you need to materialise the full n×n attention matrix.

In practice, for sequences beyond 8–16k tokens on typical hardware, the attention matrix alone exceeds GPU SRAM capacity. You spill to HBM, bandwidth becomes the bottleneck, and throughput craters.

Key insight: The quadratic cost is real — but it's a memory bandwidth problem more than a raw FLOP problem. FlashAttention (2022) exploited this distinction to deliver near-linear wall-clock scaling by tiling the computation to stay in SRAM.

Why Models Scaled Anyway

Three forces conspired to make transformers viable at scale despite the theoretical ceiling:

1. Hardware outran the theory

GPU memory bandwidth has roughly doubled every two years. A4100 → A100 → H100 → B200: each generation moved the practical breakeven point for long-context attention further out. The quadratic wall recedes as hardware improves.

2. Sparse attention patterns are sufficient

Empirically, most of the information in the attention matrix lives on the diagonal and in a small set of globally-attended "sink" tokens. Sparse attention variants — local windows, strided patterns, learned routing — achieve near-identical downstream quality at a fraction of the cost.

3. The FFN is the real workhorse

A transformer block is roughly 1/3 attention and 2/3 feed-forward network by parameter count. The FFN stores factual associations and is where most of the model's "knowledge" lives. Attention is closer to a routing mechanism — deciding which information to retrieve — than the seat of intelligence itself. That routing doesn't need to be exhaustive.

The Linear-Time Frontier

This understanding has spawned a generation of linear-complexity alternatives: Mamba (selective state spaces), RWKV (recurrence with attention-like expressiveness), and Hyena (long convolutions). Each trades the full attention matrix for a recurrence or convolution that runs in O(n) time.

The open question isn't whether they're faster — they are — but whether they preserve the in-context learning and associative recall properties that make transformers so useful. Early evidence suggests they do for most tasks, with some degradation on tasks requiring precise needle-in-a-haystack retrieval.

"The attention mechanism is a quadratic operation serving a linear purpose. The question is whether we can skip the quadratic step." — Gu & Dao, 2023

What This Means for System Design

If you're building systems today, the practical takeaways are:

  • Use FlashAttention. It's the default in every major framework now. There's no reason not to.
  • Context length is a product decision first. Don't architect for 128k tokens because you can — architect for it because your use case needs it. Longer context = more memory = higher latency = higher cost.
  • Watch the SSM space. Mamba-2 and its successors may render the quadratic discussion moot within two years. Don't over-invest in attention-specific optimisations if your inference horizon is 3+ years out.

Transformers scaled because the industry found ways to work around their theoretical limits. That's a pattern worth remembering the next time a fundamental architectural critique surfaces about whatever comes next.
